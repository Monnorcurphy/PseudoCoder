How to use this Resource

For Newbies

It is meant to be a resource for new programmers to be able to learn everything from efficiency (Big Omega, Theta, etc.), to bit manipulation, to firewalls, and everything in between. I have found that there are a lot of resources out there, including Gayle McDowell's wonderful resource (Cracking the Coding Interview), youtube videos, and even wikipedia. I have found, that I am often going through article after article, using youtube, and searching, which can be obnoxious. I wanted to make something that was easy to understand, so you can get a basic idea of how it all works from the get go, and then you can move up the chain of complexity. I will explain it simply, and then point at more advanced places where you can learn more.

For the Experienced

For those who have experience in the field but need to brush up. I will have coding tests, along with knowledge checks added to the summary section of every chapter. If you know everything and can code it all up, chances are you know everything I covered. When you have done chapter 1-4, feel free to move onto chapter 5. It is fairly advanced, covering topics of all shapes and sizes. If you can get through all of that, feel free to check out my blog, where I will be covering more advanced topics weekly in the same manner as this site.

For both

Through my explanations I want to make it simple, easy, and fun. Maybe you need to understand threading with processes, and how they work together, or you just need some way to remember which sorting algorithim is which. I will explain it, likely with an outrageous example, that makes the work simple to visualize. I also hope that in writing this I have managed to be funny. The color in the design, and in the langauge, is to make this less boring. Textbooks are great, if you are a professor, but if you are a student, you probably want something that makes you laugh while you learn, I hope to be that for you.

Reporting Problems

I will often try to explain things in a fun and easy way, which means I may make something unclear, or outright wrong. If you see a mistake, PLEASE, tell me. I will do my best to research everything, but guess what, imma human. So imma make mistakes. Since this resource is supposed to be educational, every second a mistake exists is awful, since I taught someone incorrectly. I will do my best to make sure that does not happen. Since that may not always work out, I want to make sure that people can tell me I was wrong. I appreciate anyone who corrects me, on either clarity or an error I make.
